Model Description Advantages Disadvantages
Business to Consumer (B2C) A digital health company sells its products or services directly to users, who pay for and benefit from the products or services. B2C sales cycles can be shorter. A comprehensive or complex solution may be too expensive for individuals to afford.
Consumers may be more likely to discontinue using a product or service after a short time, leading to high user turnover and customer acquisition and retention costs.
Business to Business (B2B) A digital health company sells their products or services to another company, which pays for and benefits from the product. The digital health company can capture large contracts. Enterprise purchasing cycles are often very long.
Enterprises are likely to have multiple decision makers, who can be challenging to identify and persuade.
Business to Business to Consumer (B2B2C) A subset of B2B, where the digital health company markets its products to another company, which in turn makes the product available to users. Can broaden market access to more expensive patient facing solutions. Payers and users may have conflicting ideas about what is valuable to them.
Payers and users may disagree on the amount and kinds of data that the users should share with payers.
Users who might benefit from the product may not have access to a payer.
Business to Consumer to Business (B2C2B) A digital health company provides services directly to patients, and is reimbursed by the users’ insurance companies. Removes out-of-pocket barriers for patients
More stable and predictable income than pure direct-to-consumer models 		Complex claims processing and documentation requirements
Limited to insurance-reimbursable services
Vulnerable to insurance policy changes and payor consolidation
